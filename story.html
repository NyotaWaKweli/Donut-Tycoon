My Touch

What I did to a broken donut game and why

I. The Accidental Inheritance

A few months ago, a friend on SoloLearn shared a donut clicker game. Simple stuff: a big donut emoji, some buttons, numbers going up. I left a compliment, scrolled past, didn't think much of it.

But something stuck. Not the game itself. The possibility of it. I kept thinking: this works, but it could breathe better. The numbers tick, sure, but nothing feels alive. No feedback when you click. No smoothness. No trust that your progress will survive a browser refresh.

So I went back. Not to copy — to touch.

II. What I Actually Changed (And Why)

The game logic is untouched. Same buildings, same prices, same math. I didn't reinvent the wheel, I just made it roll smoother.

The structure. The CSS opened with a syntax error that somehow still worked. The JavaScript mixed inline handlers with event listeners like it couldn't decide which era it belonged to. I cleaned it up. Just enough that the next person who opens this code won't have to squint.

The loop. setTimeout at 40ms is fine until your tab loses focus and your donuts stop accumulating while you're checking messages. I switched to requestAnimationFrame with performance.now(). The game keeps breathing even when you look away.

The feedback. alert("Bought Cursor!") is a crime against user experience. It rips you out of the game. I replaced it with floating text — little "+1" and "+0.1/s" bubbles that drift upward and vanish. You stay in the flow. The game whispers instead of shouting.

The persistence. The original had a "Save" button that basically confessed "this might not work." I added email-based save/load. Here's the thing though — it's not actually emailing anything. It just uses localStorage with your email as a key. Think of it like this: your grandpa says he wants to play too. And maybe your pet. This way they each get their own save slot without ruining your 47 factories. It's a joke, but also — it works.

III. The Details Nobody Asked For

I added focus-visible outlines for keyboard players. A backdrop-filter fallback because not every browser supports blur. Email validation with a regex that isn't perfect but catches the obvious disasters. These aren't features you list on a landing page. They're just... care. The kind of care that makes something feel finished instead of abandoned.

IV. What This Is (And Isn't)

I know clicker games are everywhere. Cookie Clicker clones are the "Hello World" of game development. I didn't make this to be original, I made it to be clear. Something a beginner can open, read, and understand. A cheat sheet with a pulse.

If you're learning JavaScript, you can look at this and see:

  • How reduce() calculates production rates
  • Why requestAnimationFrame beats setInterval
  • How to separate concerns without over-engineering
  • How to store data locally without a backend

V. What It Could Be

This isn't finished. It's not supposed to be. If you want to play with this code and make it your own, here are a few things you could try building:

  • Offline earnings. The game should count donuts even when you're logged off. Come back after a day, see what you missed. It's the whole point of idle games.
  • Achievements. "First Thousand Donuts." "Grandma Army." Little dopamine hits that keep you scrolling.
  • Prestige system. Reset everything for a permanent multiplier. The classic "do it all again, but faster" loop.
  • Visual upgrades. The donut should change as you progress: sprinkles, glaze, gold leaf. The buildings should animate. A grandma should look like she's baking.
  • Sound. The soft click of a donut. The cha-ching of a purchase. Not music — texture.
  • Dark mode toggle. Because not everyone wants a purple gradient at 2 AM.
  • Export/Import JSON. For people who want to move saves between devices without the email workaround.

VI. Why I Touch Things

I can't leave code alone. "Good enough" isn't a category I understand. I see a var where a const should live and my fingers itch. I see an alert() and I see a missed opportunity to delight someone.

This isn't about the donut. The donut is ridiculous. It's about the act of making something slightly better than you found it. About leaving fingerprints. About the stubborn belief that craft matters, even in a throwaway clicker game on a coding playground.

VII. The Actual Closing

If you're reading this and you're the friend who shared the original — thank you. I know you don't mind that I took your sketch and painted over it. The lines underneath are still yours.

If you're reading this and you're learning to code — take this. Break it. Add the offline earnings. Make the donut spin. Make it yours.

The code is yawning.
The donut is waiting.
